## 3. Purpose

The purpose of IIoT-PMO is to standardize the representation of factory equipment, industrial sensors, measurements, observations, alerts, maintenance records, time-series batches, and predicted failures in industrial predictive maintenance processes.

Version 2 extends the ontology from a static asset-sensor-observation model into a data-driven semantic architecture. The expanded ontology supports both structured sensor datasets and unstructured factory logs. It is designed for industrial environments similar to large-scale polyester production plants, where maintenance decisions depend on sensor streams, operational logs, and machine learning predictions.

## 4. Scope

IIoT-PMO v2 covers the semantic layer required for industrial IoT predictive maintenance. The current scope includes:

- asset and equipment categorization,
- sensor attachment and monitored asset relationships,
- time-stamped sensor observations,
- numeric measurement values and units,
- threshold-based alerts,
- failure-mode classification,
- maintenance logs and maintenance history,
- time-series batches for machine learning,
- predicted failures generated by ML models,
- LLM-assisted ontology population from unstructured factory logs,
- data acquisition and mapping from industrial CSV sources.

The ontology does not attempt to model PLC control logic, full production recipes, enterprise resource planning workflows, or proprietary sensor protocols. These may be addressed later through alignment with external standards and enterprise data models.

## 6. Research Integration: Ontology Population Using LLMs

IIoT-PMO v2 selects **Ontology Population using Large Language Models** as the Phase 2 research integration theme.

The reason for this selection is that large-scale factory data, especially in a large-scale production context, cannot be processed manually at realistic operational speed. Maintenance logs, shift reports, alarm texts, inspection notes, and operator comments are produced continuously and often remain unstructured. LLMs provide a practical mechanism for extracting ontological entities and relations from such text, including assets, sensors, failure symptoms, maintenance actions, temporal expressions, and candidate failure modes.

The selected approach is documented in detail in [research_review.md](research_review.md). In the proposed architecture, the FastAPI backend adds an LLM-supported ingestion service:

| API layer | Responsibility |
| --- | --- |
| `POST /ingest/sensor-csv` | Accept structured sensor data and normalize it for ontology mapping |
| `POST /ingest/factory-log` | Accept unstructured or semi-structured factory logs |
| `POST /llm/extract-ontology-candidates` | Use Ollama or GPT to extract ontology candidates from text |
| `POST /kg/populate` | Convert validated candidates into RDF triples |
| `GET /kg/assets/{asset_id}` | Retrieve semantic asset context from the knowledge graph |

The LLM is not treated as an ontology authority. It operates as an extraction assistant inside the Data Ingestion layer. Extracted candidates must be validated against IIoT-PMO classes and properties before being inserted into the knowledge graph.

## 8. Data Acquisition and Mapping

The data acquisition strategy is documented in [data_acquisition.md](data_acquisition.md). IIoT-PMO v2 uses two conceptual data sources:

| Data source | Role |
| --- | --- |
| Industrial Sensor Dataset (Kaggle) | Structured sensor time-series data for model training and ontology population |
| Factory Log Simulation | Synthetic maintenance and operations logs for LLM-based ontology population |

The mandatory mapping requirements are:

| Source field | Ontology target | Mapping |
| --- | --- | --- |
| `machine_id` | `iiot:Asset` | Each unique machine identifier becomes or resolves to an asset individual |
| `temp_c` | `iiot:MeasurementValue` | Each temperature value becomes a measurement value individual with `iiot:hasValue` and unit `degC` |

Additional fields such as `timestamp`, `sensor_id`, `vibration_mm_s`, `pressure_bar`, `failure_label`, `log_text`, and `action_taken` are mapped to observations, sensors, failure modes, maintenance logs, and datatype properties.

## 10. Core Concepts and Classification

### 10.1 Asset

An Asset is a physical or logical entity with operational relevance in an industrial environment. Assets may include production equipment, auxiliary machinery, sensors, and maintainable objects. In IIoT-PMO, Asset is the upper-level class for entities that can be monitored, maintained, or semantically identified.

### 10.2 Equipment

Equipment is a subclass of Asset representing industrial machinery that performs a production, utility, or support function. Examples include motors, pumps, compressors, conveyors, and similar devices. Equipment is the primary target of predictive maintenance analysis.

### 10.3 Sensor

A Sensor is an Asset that observes a physical property of equipment or its operational context. Sensors may measure vibration, temperature, pressure, current, flow, or other industrial signals.

### 10.4 Measurement and MeasurementValue

A Measurement is a structured representation of a value obtained from an observation. `MeasurementValue` specializes this concept for concrete numeric values mapped from data sources such as `temp_c`, `vibration_mm_s`, and `pressure_bar`.

### 10.5 Observation

An Observation is a time-stamped record generated by a sensor. It connects a sensor, a timestamp, and a measurement value. Observations are the atomic evidence units for threshold alerts and time-series prediction.

### 10.6 Alert

An Alert is a semantic notification generated when an observation indicates abnormal or risky behavior. Alerts may be threshold-based, trend-based, or model-supported.

### 10.7 FailureMode

A FailureMode is a classification of a potential or observed mechanism of equipment degradation. Examples include bearing wear, misalignment, cavitation, overheating, and imbalance.

### 10.8 MaintenanceLog

A MaintenanceLog is a textual or semi-structured maintenance record. It may include operator notes, inspection outcomes, service actions, or comments extracted from simulated factory logs. It is linked to equipment through `hasMaintenanceHistory`.

### 10.9 TimeSeriesBatch

A TimeSeriesBatch is a temporally bounded collection of observations used as input to a predictive model. It represents the bridge between RDF-based semantic modeling and GRU-RNN time-series learning.

### 10.10 PredictedFailure

A PredictedFailure represents a future failure condition predicted by a machine learning model. It is linked to a failure mode, a prediction probability, a prediction horizon, a source time-series batch, and the model that generated it.

### 10.11 MLModel

An MLModel is a computational model used for predictive analytics. In v2, `GRURNNModel` is introduced as a subclass for GRU-RNN-based time-series failure prediction.

### 10.12 LLMExtractionTask

An LLMExtractionTask represents an ontology population operation in which an LLM extracts candidate ontology individuals or relations from unstructured factory logs.

## 13. Example Scenario

Motor-01 is monitored by a vibration sensor and a temperature sensor. The vibration observation reports a value above the configured threshold, producing a critical alert. The temperature value from the `temp_c` source column is mapped to `MeasurementValue-Temp-001`.

A maintenance log states that a bearing inspection was completed earlier. A time-series batch groups recent vibration and temperature observations. A GRU-RNN model processes the batch and creates a `PredictedFailure` individual for bearing wear within a 72-hour horizon. The prediction is linked to Motor-01 through `hasPredictedFailure`, to the model through `predictedBy`, and to the source batch through `derivedFromBatch`.

## 14. Non-Functional Requirements

- The ontology must be readable in Protege.
- The ontology must use stable and descriptive URI identifiers.
- The ontology must be serializable in Turtle format.
- Class and property names must follow consistent UpperCamelCase and lowerCamelCase conventions.
- LLM-generated individuals must be validated before graph insertion.
- Time-series mappings must preserve source timestamps and units.
- Prediction outputs must preserve traceability to the source batch and ML model.
- The ontology should remain extensible toward SOSA/SSN, QUDT, ISO 14224, and MIMOSA/OpenO&M alignment.

## 15. Reuse and Alignment Strategy

The v2 ontology remains self-contained for classroom readability and Protege inspection. Future versions should consider alignment with:

- SOSA/SSN for sensor and observation semantics,
- QUDT or OM for measurement units,
- ISO 14224 for equipment reliability and failure data,
- MIMOSA/OpenO&M for asset lifecycle integration,
- ML-Schema or similar vocabularies for machine learning metadata,
- PROV-O for provenance of LLM extraction and data ingestion.

## 16. Validation Plan

Validation will be performed by:

1. loading `ontology/iiot-pmo-v2.ttl` in Protege,
2. checking class hierarchy and property consistency,
3. writing SPARQL queries for each competency question,
4. validating `machine_id` to `Asset` and `temp_c` to `MeasurementValue` mappings,
5. creating sample RDF triples from Kaggle-style sensor rows,
6. creating sample RDF triples from simulated factory logs,
7. checking traceability from `PredictedFailure` to `TimeSeriesBatch` and `MLModel`,
8. reviewing LLM extraction candidates with a human-in-the-loop approval process.
